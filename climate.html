CLIMATE PROJECT

FAVORITES

A lift attendant looks towards the 1,100 acres of conservation land while waiting for skiers to unload at Sunday River ski resort in Newry, Maine. Karolyn Castaldo, the Director of Communications, explains that the resort has chosen not to develop the land since it would be a “tremendous environmental impact to start doing that.” She added, “Business levels aren’t really showing that [they] need that space yet.” The ski resort hopes to ensure “that [they] can keep that nature wild out there for as long as [they] can.” February 9, 2020.

I completed this senior project to fullfill the Framingham State University Honors Program. The series of photographs and interviews focuses on climate change in the Northeast and how New Englanders are responding to it. 

An HKD snow gun extends over a Sunday River ski trail. According to Castaldo, “Probably the last 10 years we’ve had a greater focus on snowmaking efficiency. It’s our single largest energy use at the resort, so it’s something that we really need to be focusing on in terms of sustainability.” February 9, 2020.

An HKD snowmaking fan sits alongside a ski trail. “We have a couple of different kinds at the resort,” Castaldo says, “but the base premise is that [the snow makers] use more water and less air, because the air compressors use more energy than the water does.” February 9, 2020.

The Institute of Contemporary Art stands at the edge of Boston Harbor. The Boston Seaport is already flood-prone during major storms. Projected rising sea-levels greatly increase that risk. February 23, 2020.

Cranes are situated on a construction site, as new developments are built along the harbor. Construction continues throughout the Seaport District despite the threat of flooding due to rising sea-levels. February 23, 2020.

Professor Aviva Liebert, holding a taxidermy bee collection, explains how changes in temperature caused by climate change can cause certain species of bees to come out of hibernation early. This advantage can cause them to outcompete other species, resulting in the losing species dying off. “The earlier the temperatures are warm,” Professor Liebert says, “the quicker they can get started and then they outcompete everyone else. What it looks like is there used to be something like 11 different species and we’re now down to, in the early part of this season, maybe two or three and at the end of the season only one. So, the diversity is really seeing changes.” Framingham State University, March 15, 2020.

Recently planted trees line a sidewalk in Boston. Tree canopy is vital for mitigating the effects of Climate Change. Shade cast from trees reduces urban heat, which tends to decrease reliance on air-conditioning. Additionally, root systems are important for preventing stormwater runoff as seawaters continue to rise. March 2, 2020.

A posted sign marks an area were a green space is being restored. Areas like these are vital for the city’s healthy tree canopy. March 2, 2020.
